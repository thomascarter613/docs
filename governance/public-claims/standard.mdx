# Public claim standard

The Public Claim Standard defines how claims should be reviewed before they appear in public docs, sales material, publishing, or authority assets.

A public claim is any statement that a reader may treat as factual proof, capability, guarantee, or commitment.

---

## Purpose

This standard exists to:

- protect trust
- avoid overstatement
- reduce legal and reputational risk
- keep narrative grounded
- improve sales accuracy
- prevent AI-generated exaggeration

---

## Public claim examples

Examples include:

- product capability claims
- client outcome claims
- market claims
- pricing claims
- security claims
- performance claims
- implementation timeline claims
- competitive claims
- governance maturity claims

---

## Claim review questions

Ask:

- Is this true?
- Can we prove it?
- Is it current?
- Is it specific?
- Is it overstated?
- Does it imply a guarantee?
- Does it require evidence?
- Does it expose private information?
- Was it generated by AI?
- Does it need human verification?

---

## Claim evidence levels

| Evidence level | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| None | Do not publish as fact |
| Weak | Mark as hypothesis or observation |
| Moderate | Use cautiously with context |
| Strong | Safe to state clearly |
| Verified | Safe to use as canonical claim |

---

## Safer wording

Unsafe:

> We guarantee AI will reduce your admin workload by 50%.

Safer:

> We look for repetitive administrative workflows where automation may reduce manual effort, missed follow-up, and duplicated work.

Unsafe:

> Monad-Workspace is the best AI operating system.

Safer:

> Monad-Workspace is being designed as a local-first, governance-grade workspace for AI-assisted execution.

---

## Definition of done

The Public Claim Standard is working when public material is clear, credible, and grounded without overstating what has been proven.
