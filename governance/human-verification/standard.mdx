# Human verification standard

The Human Verification Standard defines what must be reviewed by a human before it becomes accepted, published, sent, or executed.

AI can assist. The founder approves.

---

## Purpose

Human verification exists to:

- protect trust
- reduce AI hallucination risk
- prevent accidental commitments
- protect clients
- preserve decision quality
- avoid unsupported public claims
- keep governance under human control

---

## Requires human verification

Human verification is required for:

- accepted ADRs
- governance changes
- pricing changes
- public claims
- client-facing commitments
- legal or financial conclusions
- major platform decisions
- major positioning changes
- sensitive data handling
- final sales proposals
- published authority assets
- AI-generated doctrine

---

## Does not usually require human verification

Routine AI assistance may not require special verification when it involves:

- formatting
- summarizing already-approved text
- fixing typos
- generating draft outlines
- reorganizing non-canonical notes
- preparing first drafts clearly marked as drafts

---

## Verification questions

Before approval, ask:

- Is this true?
- Is this safe?
- Is this current?
- Is this supported by evidence?
- Does this create a commitment?
- Does this change doctrine?
- Does this affect a client?
- Does this affect public trust?
- Is this AI-generated content being treated as final?

---

## Approval states

| State | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Draft | Not approved |
| Review required | Needs human review |
| Approved | Human verified |
| Rejected | Not accepted |
| Superseded | Replaced by later verified content |

---

## Definition of done

Human verification is working when AI can accelerate work without silently becoming the source of authority.
