# Assumption Extraction Guide

This guide explains how to extract assumptions from Founder Log entries.

Assumptions are beliefs the business is acting on without complete proof. They are not bad. They are dangerous only when they remain invisible.

The Founder Log should make assumptions visible, testable, and reviewable.

---

## What counts as an assumption?

An assumption is a belief that influences action but still needs evidence.

Examples:

- SMBs will pay for AI implementation if framed around operational relief.
- A founder-led consulting offer can produce reusable SaaS requirements.
- Prospects care more about outcomes than tooling.
- A low-code workflow can be replaced later by a custom platform module.
- Founder memory can be converted into AI Cofounder context effectively.

---

## Assumption extraction questions

When reading a log entry, ask:

- What are we treating as true?
- What must be true for this plan to work?
- What belief is driving this decision?
- What has not been proven yet?
- What would make this direction fail?
- What should the AI Cofounder avoid assuming too strongly?

If the answer affects execution, extract the assumption.

---

## Assumption format

Use this format.

```md
## Assumption

[Write the assumption as a testable belief.]

## Area

Strategy / Pipeline / Delivery / Platform / Governance / Narrative / Operations

## Why it matters

[Explain what depends on this being true.]

## Current evidence

[What evidence currently supports it?]

## Counterevidence

[What evidence weakens it?]

## Confidence

Low / Medium / High

## Test

[How can this assumption be tested?]

## Decision impact

[What decisions depend on this assumption?]

## Review date

[When should this be checked again?]
````

---

## Writing good assumptions

Weak assumption:

> People want automation.

Better assumption:

> Local service businesses with repetitive administrative workflows may buy automation services when the offer is tied to measurable time savings.

A good assumption is:

* specific
* testable
* tied to a business area
* connected to decisions
* assigned a confidence level
* reviewable

---

## Confidence levels

| Confidence | Meaning                                                                                      |
| ---------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Low        | Mostly intuition or weak evidence                                                            |
| Medium     | Some evidence exists, but more testing is needed                                             |
| High       | Repeated evidence supports the belief, but it should still be revisited if conditions change |

Do not use high confidence merely because an assumption feels obvious.

---

## Test types

Assumptions can be tested through:

* prospect conversations
* landing page response
* sales objections
* paid pilots
* delivery outcomes
* workflow measurements
* pricing experiments
* content engagement
* implementation prototypes
* manual concierge tests

Choose the smallest useful test.

## Example

Raw log note:

> It seems like the consulting work should create the product roadmap. Every client delivery should reveal a reusable module or template.

Extracted assumption:

```md
## Assumption

Founder-led AI implementation consulting can generate reusable platform requirements for the future SaaS product.

## Area

Platform / Delivery

## Why it matters

The business model depends on consulting producing both revenue and product intelligence.

## Current evidence

The planned delivery model is designed to extract reusable workflows, templates, and implementation patterns.

## Counterevidence

No completed client engagements have proven this loop yet.

## Confidence

Medium

## Test

After each client engagement, identify whether at least one reusable asset, template, or product requirement was created.

## Decision impact

This assumption supports the SaaS-plus-consulting model.

## Review date

Monthly review after the first three engagements.
```

---

## Assumption lifecycle

Assumptions move through these states:

1. **Identified** — the assumption has been noticed.
2. **Defined** — the assumption is written clearly.
3. **Testable** — a test has been assigned.
4. **Testing** — evidence is being gathered.
5. **Confirmed** — evidence supports the assumption.
6. **Weakened** — evidence is mixed or negative.
7. **Rejected** — the assumption should no longer guide action.
8. **Converted** — the assumption became a decision, rule, or doctrine.

---

## Weekly review process

During weekly review:

1. Scan daily logs for implied beliefs.
2. Extract assumptions that affect decisions.
3. Assign confidence levels.
4. Connect assumptions to decisions.
5. Define tests.
6. Mark assumptions that need monthly review.
7. Promote confirmed assumptions into doctrine only after repeated evidence.

---

## Assumption quality checklist

An assumption is ready to track when:

* it is written as a testable belief
* it identifies what depends on it
* it has evidence and counterevidence
* it has a confidence level
* it has a test
* it has a review date
* it is connected to a business area

Invisible assumptions create hidden risk. Visible assumptions create learning.
